Brawl Heaven

Brawl Heaven is a platform fighter where you can draw. The objective is to knock your opponent off the map, and you've got the creative tools to do it — drawn shapes can become a black hole, an explosion, a smoke screen, or a healing zone.

Development

Brawl Heaven was my third project, and it pushed me to grow a lot in terms of planning and scope management.

I focused on building unique mechanics where drawn shapes turned into different gameplay effects — a black hole that pulls in opponents, an explosion that knocks players away, a smoke screen for cover, and a healing zone for recovery. Designing these taught me about building abilities that change how players approach a fight.

The biggest lesson from this project was the importance of scoping and polish. The core gameplay worked, but I realized how crucial it is to refine the feel of mechanics and focus on usability — balancing the drawing mechanic alongside combat was genuinely challenging.

Looking back, I'd improve the project by optimizing the controls, smoothing the gameplay flow, and adding more polish to visuals and feedback. Despite its flaws, Brawl Heaven gave me valuable experience in experimental design, scope management, and iteration that I've carried into later projects.

Explanation

This script controls the Painter character's ultimate ability — drawing a custom shape in real time that becomes a mesh with gameplay properties. When the ultimate activates:

  • The player's movement pauses and a cursor system spawns, letting them draw with the analog stick
  • The script tracks drawn points and makes sure the loop closes properly before generating a mesh
  • A custom triangulation algorithm converts the drawn shape into a 2D mesh with UVs and colliders
  • The mesh is assigned one of several properties (explosion, smoke screen, black hole, healing zone) based on player input
  • Particle systems and materials are applied to visually represent the chosen effect

This was one of the most technically challenging parts of the project — it meant combining input handling, real-time mesh generation, collider creation, and custom geometry logic into a single system.

Everything I learned

Local multiplayer

First time building a proper local multiplayer game, and I learned how important good code structure is for separating players and controllers.

Messy code

My player controller wasn't modular or scalable, which led to bugs and slower development since every script did a little of everything.

Animations

First time implementing animations with proper collisions — afterwards I learned about animation events, which would've made timing and collisions much easier.

Scope creep

We tried to add too much before the fundamental game was done, which hindered development and taught me the importance of proper planning and documentation.
